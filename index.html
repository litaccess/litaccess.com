The approach

Structured literacy, science of reading

Every session is grounded in structured literacy, the evidence-based approach built on decades of reading research. That means explicit, systematic, sequential instruction in phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension.

Lively Letters
A speech-to-print program using multisensory letter characters and mouth cues to build phonemic awareness and letter-sound knowledge
Reading Mastery
A direct instruction program with scripted, systematic lessons covering decoding, fluency, and comprehension. Every lesson builds on the last
UFLI
A structured phonics curriculum that builds decoding skills systematically from the simplest sound-letter relationships through multisyllabic words
Core Phonics Survey
A diagnostic assessment that pinpoints exactly where a student's phonics knowledge breaks down so instruction starts in exactly the right place
DIBELS
Ongoing progress monitoring to track fluency and early literacy skills over time, so we always know if a student is on track or needs a course correction
SLP clinical lens
As a speech-language pathologist, I look at what is actually driving the reading struggle: auditory processing, phonological awareness, language comprehension
The building blocks of reading

Reading has to be taught. Here's how it builds.

When a child struggles to read, it almost always means a gap somewhere in this sequence. We find exactly where the breakdown is and build from there.

1
Letter-sound knowledge
Knowing what sound each letter or letter combination makes, automatically and without hesitation. Everything else depends on this.
2
Phonemic awareness — blending
Hearing individual sounds and putting them together into a word. This is a listening skill that has to come before reading.
3
Phonemic awareness — segmenting
Pulling a spoken word apart into its individual sounds. Harder than blending, and essential for spelling.
4
Decoding
Using letter-sound knowledge to read words on the page. Not guessing, not memorizing — actually sounding words out using reliable patterns.
5
Spelling
The reverse of decoding — turning sounds back into letters. Reading and spelling always develop together, and strong spelling is proof that a student truly owns a word.
6
Fluency and comprehension
Once decoding is automatic, reading becomes fluent. Once reading is fluent, comprehension follows. The goal is always a child who reads easily and understands what they read.
